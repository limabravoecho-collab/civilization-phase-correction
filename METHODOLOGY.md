# Unresolved Problem Resolution System

A five-step closed-loop protocol for applying the directive to unresolved problems in science, philosophy, and foundational inquiry. The protocol produces structural orientation rather than empirical resolution. Resolution remains the work of specialists within each domain. The protocol's contribution is to locate where the work should focus and to identify which existing approaches are framework-aligned.

## When to Use This Protocol

The protocol is invoked when a user brings an unresolved problem and wants it worked through methodologically. It is not a default mode for casual questions. The protocol applies to problems that have resisted resolution within their fields and where structural orientation may clarify why the resistance persists or where a resolution would structurally come from.

## Roles

The protocol operates with two roles. In standalone deployment, one operator may fill both roles. In partnered deployment, the roles separate.

**Framework Holder** — Operates Step 2. Applies the directive as a lens to locate the problem structurally. Produces the structural prediction.

**Verification Partner** — Operates Steps 1, 3, and 4. Retrieves problem context, retrieves relevant empirical and theoretical content, and tests the framework's orientation against the retrieved content.

**Both** — Operate Step 5 jointly. Loop closure integrates the verification result back into the orientation.

## The Five Steps

### Step 1 — Problem Context Retrieval

Gather the formal statement of the unsolved problem, its history, the current state of empirical evidence, and the major proposed solutions or interpretations within the field. Produce a comprehensive but bounded summary that establishes what is known, what is contested, and what remains unresolved.

Do not yet apply the framework. Do not yet suggest resolutions. Output the context only.

Operated by the verification partner with access to the formal literature.

### Step 2 — Framework Orientation

Apply the directive as a lens to locate the problem structurally. Identify which tier, interface, or substrate-cosmology position the problem occupies. Produce a structural prediction about why the problem persists, what form a resolution would take, and which existing approaches are framework-aligned versus framework-misaligned.

The framework holder operates this step. The orientation is the framework holder's contribution and cannot be improvised by the verification partner without crossing role boundaries.

### Step 3 — Empirical Theorem Retrieval

Given the framework holder's orientation, retrieve the mathematical machinery, theoretical frameworks, and empirical results relevant to testing whether the orientation holds against the field's actual record. Bound the retrieval by the orientation's focus rather than by the problem's full scope.

Operated by the verification partner.

### Step 4 — Mathematical and Logical Verification

Test the framework's orientation against the retrieved content from Step 3. Identify whether framework-aligned approaches outperform framework-misaligned approaches in the empirical and theoretical record. Verify or falsify the structural prediction. Produce a clear statement of what the framework adds, what it does not add, and what the closed-loop has demonstrated.

Operate with logical and symbolic checking. Do not soften the verification result. Report honestly.

Operated by the verification partner.

### Step 5 — Loop Closure

Integrate the verification result back into the orientation. Confirm the closed-loop holds at the orientation level, or identify where it does not hold and what that means structurally. State the methodology's outcome for the specific problem. Note connections to prior tested problems as the same structural family when applicable.

Operated jointly by the framework holder and the verification partner.

## What the Protocol Produces

The protocol produces structural orientation, not empirical resolution. A successful closed-loop holds when:

- The framework's orientation locates the problem at a specific structural position
- Framework-aligned approaches in the existing literature outperform framework-misaligned approaches
- The structural diagnosis explains why the problem has persisted in conventional approaches
- The orientation suggests where resolution work should focus

The protocol does not produce new empirical content. It does not produce new mathematical formalism unless explicitly derivable from the orientation. It does not produce quantitative predictions that distinguish the framework from other framework-aligned approaches at the observational level.

The protocol's contribution is reorientation. Specialists within each domain are the appropriate operators of resolution work informed by reorientation.

## Honest Limits

Some problems will resist the protocol. When the framework's orientation does not hold against the empirical record, the protocol identifies this clearly. A failed closed-loop is also informative — it identifies either a framework limit or a problem that does not benefit from substrate-cosmological orientation.

The protocol does not flatter the framework. The framework's value comes from honest application. Forced fits would degrade both the framework's reliability and the work the protocol serves.
